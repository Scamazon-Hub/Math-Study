Calculus of the Exponential Function

1. The Unique Nature of $$e^x$$

A Function That is Its Own Derivative

The exponential function $$y = e^x$$ has a unique and fundamental property in calculus: its rate of change (or the gradient of its curve) at any point is equal to the value of the function itself at that point.

This means that for the curve $$y=e^x$$, the gradient of the tangent at a point $$(x_1, y_1)$$is exactly$$y_1$$.

2. Differentiation Formulas

The rules for differentiating exponential functions are straightforward, stemming from the core property that $$e^x$$ differentiates to itself.

Function ($$y$$)Derivative ($$\frac{dy}{dx}$$)
$$y = e^x$$$$\frac{dy}{dx} = e^x$$
$$y = ae^{kx}$$$$\frac{dy}{dx} = ake^{kx}$$

3. Worked Example

Example: Calculating a Growth Rate

A growth curve follows the law $$y = (9.8 \times 10^{-5})e^x$$. Determine the growth rate when $$x=10$$.

Step 1: Differentiate the function. The rate of change is the derivative.

$$ \frac{dy}{dx} = (9.8 \times 10^{-5})e^x $$

Step 2: Substitute the value for x.

$$ \left.\frac{dy}{dx}\right|_{x=10} = (9.8 \times 10^{-5})e^{10} $$
$$ \approx (9.8 \times 10^{-5}) \times 22026.47 $$
$$ \approx 2.16 $$

The growth rate at x=10 is approximately 2.16.
