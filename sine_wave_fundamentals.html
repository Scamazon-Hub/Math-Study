Sine Wave Fundamentals

1. The Anatomy of a Sine Wave

The Universal Waveform

Sinusoidal waves (or sinusoids) are fundamental in engineering and science, describing everything from AC electricity and mechanical vibrations to sound and light waves.

The instantaneous value (y) of a waveform at any given time (t) can be described by the following equation:

$$ y = A\sin(\omega t + \phi) $$

TermSymbolDescription
Amplitude$$A$$The maximum value or peak of the wave from its center position.
Angular Frequency$$\omega$$How fast the wave oscillates in radians per second (rad/s). It's related to frequency by $$\omega = 2\pi f$$.
Period$$T$$The time taken to complete one full cycle, in seconds (s). $$T = 1/f = 2\pi/\omega$$.
Frequency$$f$$The number of complete cycles per second, measured in Hertz (Hz).
Phase Angle$$\phi$$The starting angle of the wave at t=0, in radians. It represents a horizontal shift.

2. Worked Example: Deconstructing a Waveform

An AC current is given by the equation $$i = 43\sin(50\pi t + 0.6)$$. Find its key characteristics.

By comparing the equation to the general form $$y = A\sin(\omega t + \phi)$$, we can identify the values directly:

a) Amplitude (A): $$ A = 43 \text{ A} $$

b) Angular Frequency ($$\omega$$): $$ \omega = 50\pi \text{ rad/s} $$

c) Frequency (f): $$ f = \frac{\omega}{2\pi} = \frac{50\pi}{2\pi} = 25 \text{ Hz} $$

d) Period (T): $$ T = \frac{1}{f} = \frac{1}{25} = 0.04 \text{ s} = 40 \text{ ms} $$

e) Phase Angle ($$\phi$$): $$ \phi = 0.6 \text{ rad} $$ (or $$ \approx 34.4^\circ $$)

3. The Cosine Wave Relationship

Just a Phase Shift

A cosine wave has the exact same shape as a sine wave, but it is shifted to the left by 90Â° ($$\pi/2$$ radians). A cosine wave starts at its maximum value, whereas a sine wave starts at zero.

$$ \cos(x) = \sin(x + \frac{\pi}{2}) $$

4. Important Waveform Values

Beyond the Peak

In many engineering applications, especially AC circuits, we are interested in the "effective" or average value of a wave, not just its peak amplitude.
  • RMS (Root Mean Square) Value: This is the effective value of the waveform. It's the equivalent DC value that would deliver the same amount of power. For a sinusoid, the formula is: $$ A_{RMS} = \frac{A}{\sqrt{2}} \approx 0.707A $$
  • Average Value: The true average over a full cycle is zero. The "average value" usually refers to the average over half a cycle (the average of the rectified wave). For a sinusoid, the formula is: $$ A_{avg} = \frac{2A}{\pi} \approx 0.637A $$

For the wave $$i = 43\sin(50\pi t + 0.6)$$, find the RMS and average current.

1. RMS Current:
$$ I_{RMS} = \frac{43}{\sqrt{2}} \approx 30.4 \text{ A} $$

2. Average Current:
$$ I_{avg} = \frac{2 \times 43}{\pi} \approx 27.4 \text{ A} $$
