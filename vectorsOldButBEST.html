Vectors

Vector Fundamentals

Scalars vs. Vectors

  • A Scalar is a quantity that has magnitude (size) only. Examples: speed, distance, mass, time.
  • A Vector is a quantity that has both magnitude and direction. Examples: velocity, force, displacement.

Scalar Multiplication

Definition

Multiplying a vector by a scalar changes its magnitude. If the scalar is negative, it also reverses the vector's direction. The vector remains parallel to its original line of action.

Unit Vectors (i, j, k)

A unit vector has a magnitude of 1 and is used to specify a direction. The special unit vectors $$\mathbf{i}$$, $$\mathbf{j}$$, and $$\mathbf{k}$$ represent the directions of the x, y, and z axes in a Cartesian coordinate system.

Vector Addition and Subtraction

Triangle Rule

How it Works

To add two vectors $$\mathbf{a}$$ and $$\mathbf{b}$$, place the start of vector $$\mathbf{b}$$ at the end of vector $$\mathbf{a}$$. The sum, or resultant vector, is obtained by the third line which completes the triangle.

Resolving Vectors into Components

When to Use

To analyze forces or velocities that don't act purely horizontally or vertically. Any vector can be broken down into two perpendicular components, which is essential for adding multiple vectors together by components.

For a vector $$\mathbf{F}$$ with magnitude $$|\mathbf{F}|$$ at an angle $$\theta$$ to the horizontal:

Horizontal Component ($$F_1$$):

$$ F_1 = |\mathbf{F}|\cos\theta $$

Vertical Component ($$F_2$$):

$$ F_2 = |\mathbf{F}|\sin\theta $$

Example: Express a force of 10 N acting at 40° to the horizontal in terms of horizontal and vertical components.

Horizontal Component:

$$ F_1 = 10 \times \cos(40^\circ) \approx 7.66 \text{ N} $$

Vertical Component:

$$ F_2 = 10 \times \sin(40^\circ) \approx 6.43 \text{ N} $$

Result in component form: $$ \mathbf{F} = (7.66\mathbf{i} + 6.43\mathbf{j}) \text{ N} $$

Example: Vector $$\mathbf{a}$$ has magnitude 10. Vector $$\mathbf{b}$$ has magnitude 20 and is at an angle of 30° to $$\mathbf{a}$$. Find the magnitude of $$\mathbf{a}+\mathbf{b}$$ using the triangle rule.

Step 1: Draw the vector triangle. By placing the vectors tip-to-tail, the angle inside the triangle between them is $$180^\circ - 30^\circ = 150^\circ$$.

Step 2: Use the Cosine Rule to find the magnitude of the resultant vector.

$$ |\mathbf{a}+\mathbf{b}|^2 = |\mathbf{a}|^2 + |\mathbf{b}|^2 - 2|\mathbf{a}||\mathbf{b}|\cos(150^\circ) $$ $$ |\mathbf{a}+\mathbf{b}|^2 = 10^2 + 20^2 - 2(10)(20)\cos(150^\circ) $$ $$ |\mathbf{a}+\mathbf{b}|^2 = 100 + 400 - 400(-0.866) = 846.41 $$ $$ |\mathbf{a}+\mathbf{b}| = \sqrt{846.41} \approx 29.1 $$

Parallelogram Rule

How it Works

An alternative rule for adding two vectors. Place the start points of vectors $$\mathbf{a}$$ and $$\mathbf{b}$$ together, then complete the parallelogram. The diagonal of the parallelogram from the starting point represents the sum of $$\mathbf{a}$$ and $$\mathbf{b}$$.

Example: An object is acted upon by two forces. One is 10N acting horizontally to the right. The other is 20N acting at 60° to the horizontal to the left. Find the resultant force R.

Step 1: Draw the parallelogram. The angle between the two force vectors is $$180^\circ - 60^\circ = 120^\circ$$. In the vector triangle used for calculation, the corresponding angle is $$180^\circ - 120^\circ = 60^\circ$$.

Step 2: Use the Cosine Rule to find the magnitude of R.

$$ |\mathbf{R}|^2 = 10^2 + 20^2 - 2(10)(20)\cos(60^\circ) $$ $$ |\mathbf{R}|^2 = 100 + 400 - 400(0.5) = 300 $$ $$ |\mathbf{R}| = \sqrt(300) \approx 17.3 \text{ N} $$

Step 3: Use the Cosine Rule again to find the direction.

Let $$\theta$$ be the angle between the resultant R and the 20N force.

$$ \cos\theta = \frac{|\mathbf{R}|^2 + 20^2 - 10^2}{2 \times |\mathbf{R}| \times 20} = \frac{300 + 400 - 100}{2 \times \sqrt{300} \times 20} = \frac{600}{692.8} \approx 0.866 $$ $$ \theta = \cos^{-1}(0.866) = 30^\circ $$

Position Vectors, Unit Vectors & Direction Cosines

Position Vectors

The vector $$\vec{OP} = \mathbf{r}$$ specifies the position of a point P relative to the origin O and is called the position vector of P. Using the triangle rule, it can be expressed in terms of its components along the axes.

For a point P(a,b), the position vector is given by:

$$ \mathbf{r} = a\mathbf{i} + b\mathbf{j} $$

Direction Cosines

The direction cosines of a vector are the cosines of the angles the vector makes with the x and y axes ($$\alpha$$ and $$\beta$$ respectively). They are also the components of the unit vector in the direction of the main vector.

$$ \cos\alpha = \frac{a}{|\mathbf{r}|}, \quad \cos\beta = \frac{b}{|\mathbf{r}|} $$

Example: The position vector of a point is P(4,7). Determine the magnitude of the vector, its direction cosines, and the direction angles.

Step 1: Write the vector in component form and find its magnitude.
The vector is $$ \mathbf{r} = 4\mathbf{i} + 7\mathbf{j} $$.

$$ |\mathbf{r}| = \sqrt{4^2 + 7^2} = \sqrt{16 + 49} = \sqrt{65} \approx 8.06 $$

Step 2: Find the direction cosines.
The direction cosines are the components of the unit vector $$\hat{\mathbf{r}}$$.

$$ \cos\alpha = \frac{4}{\sqrt{65}}, \quad \cos\beta = \frac{7}{\sqrt{65}} $$

Step 3: Find the direction angles ($$\alpha$$, $$\beta$$) by taking the inverse cosine.

$$ \alpha = \arccos\left(\frac{4}{\sqrt{65}}\right) \approx 60.26^\circ $$
$$ \beta = \arccos\left(\frac{7}{\sqrt{65}}\right) \approx 29.74^\circ $$
