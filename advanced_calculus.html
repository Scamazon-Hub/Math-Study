Advanced Calculus

1. Further Integration Techniques

When to Use

Use these methods for integrals that cannot be solved directly from standard tables, especially for products of functions or composite functions that look like the result of the chain rule.

Integration by Parts

This technique is used to integrate the product of two functions. It effectively reverses the product rule for differentiation.

Formula: $$ \int u \frac{dv}{dx} dx = uv - \int v \frac{du}{dx} dx $$.

Example: Find $$ \int x e^{x} \,dx $$

Step 1: Choose u and dv/dx. A good rule of thumb is LATE (Logs, Algebra, Trig, Exponential) for choosing 'u'. Here, 'x' is algebraic, so we choose it.
Let $$u=x$$ and $$\frac{dv}{dx}=e^{x}$$.

Step 2: Differentiate u and integrate dv/dx.
$$ \frac{du}{dx}=1 $$ and $$v= \int e^{x} dx = e^{x}$$

Step 3: Apply the formula.
$$ \int x e^{x} dx = (x)(e^x) - \int (e^x)(1) dx $$

Step 4: Solve the remaining integral.
$$ = xe^{x} - e^{x} + C $$

Final Answer: $$ e^x(x-1) + C $$

Integration by Substitution

This technique is used to simplify an integral by changing the variable of integration. It is the reverse of the chain rule for differentiation.

Example: Find $$ \int 2x \sqrt{1+x^2} \,dx $$

Step 1: Choose a substitution 'u'. Let 'u' be the inner function. Here, that is the term inside the square root.
Let $$u = 1+x^2$$.

Step 2: Find du/dx and rearrange for dx.
$$ \frac{du}{dx} = 2x \implies du = 2x \,dx $$

Step 3: Substitute u and du into the integral.
Notice that $$2x \,dx$$ in the original integral can be replaced entirely by $$du$$.
$$ \int \sqrt{1+x^2} (2x \,dx) = \int \sqrt{u} \,du = \int u^{1/2} \,du $$

Step 4: Integrate with respect to u.
$$ \int u^{1/2} \,du = \frac{u^{3/2}}{3/2} + C = \frac{2}{3}u^{3/2} + C $$

Step 5: Substitute x back into the expression.
$$ = \frac{2}{3}(1+x^2)^{3/2} + C $$

2. First-Order Differential Equations

What are they?

These equations model dynamic systems where the rate of change of a quantity depends on the quantity itself. They are used in heat transfer, simple RC/RL circuits, and population models. We will cover two common solution methods.

Separation of Variables

Use for equations that can be written in the form $$ \frac{dy}{dx} = f(x)g(y) $$. The strategy is to move all y-terms to one side and all x-terms to the other, then integrate both sides.

Example: Solve $$ \frac{dy}{dx} = 3x^2y $$

Step 1: Separate the variables.
$$ \frac{1}{y} dy = 3x^2 dx $$

Step 2: Integrate both sides.
$$ \int \frac{1}{y} dy = \int 3x^2 dx $$
$$ \ln|y| = x^3 + C $$

Step 3: Solve for y.
$$ y = e^{x^3 + C} = e^{x^3}e^C $$
Since $$e^C$$ is just another constant, we can call it 'A'.

Final Answer: $$ y = Ae^{x^3} $$

Integrating Factor Method

Use for linear equations that can be written in the form $$ \frac{dy}{dx} + P(x)y = Q(x) $$.

Integrating Factor (IF): $$ \text{IF} = e^{\int P(x)dx} $$

General Solution: $$ y \times \text{IF} = \int Q(x) \times \text{IF} \, dx $$

Example: Solve $$ \frac{dy}{dx} + 2y = 4x $$

Step 1: Identify P(x) and find the Integrating Factor (IF).
Comparing to the standard form, $$P(x) = 2$$.
$$ \text{IF} = e^{\int 2 dx} = e^{2x} $$.

Step 2: Apply the general solution formula.
$$ y e^{2x} = \int 4x e^{2x} dx $$

Step 3: Solve the integral on the right side. This requires integration by parts.
$$ \int 4xe^{2x} dx = 2xe^{2x} - e^{2x} + C $$

Step 4: Rearrange to solve for y.
$$ y = \frac{2xe^{2x} - e^{2x} + C}{e^{2x}} $$

Final Answer: $$ y = 2x - 1 + Ce^{-2x} $$

3. Second-Order Homogeneous Differential Equations

When to Use

To model systems like mechanical oscillations (springs, pendulums) and RLC circuits. We focus on linear equations with constant coefficients.

For an equation of the form $$ a\frac{d^2y}{dx^2} + b\frac{dy}{dx} + cy = 0 $$, we first form the **Auxiliary Equation**: $$ ak^2 + bk + c = 0 $$.

The general solution for y depends on the nature of the roots (k) of this quadratic equation.

  • Case 1: Real, distinct roots ($$k_1, k_2$$)
    Solution: $$ y = Ae^{k_1x} + Be^{k_2x} $$
  • Case 2: Real, repeated roots ($$k$$)
    Solution: $$ y = (Ax+B)e^{kx} $$
  • Case 3: Complex roots ($$\alpha \pm j\beta$$)
    Solution: $$ y = e^{\alpha x}(A\cos(\beta x) + B\sin(\beta x)) $$

Example: Solve $$ \frac{d^2y}{dx^2} + 5\frac{dy}{dx} + 4y = 0 $$

Step 1: Write the auxiliary equation.
$$ k^2 + 5k + 4 = 0 $$

Step 2: Solve for the roots (k).
$$ (k+1)(k+4) = 0 $$
The roots are $$ k_1 = -1 $$ and $$ k_2 = -4 $$.

Step 3: Identify the case and write the general solution.
The roots are real and distinct (Case 1).

Final Answer: $$ y = Ae^{-x} + Be^{-4x} $$
