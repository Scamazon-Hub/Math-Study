The Chain Rule (Function of a Function)

1. Understanding Cascade Relationships

What is a Cascade Relationship?

In many systems, variables are linked indirectly. For example, a variable $$y$$ might depend on $$z$$, which in turn depends on $$x$$. This creates a chain or 'cascade' where changing $$x$$ affects $$z$$, which then affects $$y$$. We can represent this as two linked functions: $$y=f(z)$$ and $$z=f(x)$$.

A Physical Analogy

Consider a heating process: the final temperature rise depends on the heat input, which itself depends on the rate of fuel consumption. This is a perfect real-world example of a cascade relationship.

2. The Chain Rule Formula

The Chain Rule allows us to find the overall rate of change, $$\frac{dy}{dx}$$, by multiplying the rates of change of the individual functions in the cascade.

$$ \frac{dy}{dx} = \frac{dy}{dz} \times \frac{dz}{dx} $$

You can think of this as the '$$dz$$' terms on the right-hand side 'cancelling out' to leave $$\frac{dy}{dx}$$. While not a true cancellation, it's a useful way to remember the formula's structure.

3. Worked Example

Example: Given the relationships $$y=z^2$$ and $$z=x^3$$, find the overall rate of change $$\frac{dy}{dx}$$.

Method 1: Direct Substitution

First, we can create a single function linking y and x by substituting $$z=x^3$$ into $$y=z^2$$.

$$ y = (x^3)^2 = x^6 $$

Now, we differentiate this new function directly:

$$ \frac{dy}{dx} = 6x^5 $$

Method 2: Using the Chain Rule

Step 1: Differentiate each part of the cascade separately.

For $$y=z^2$$, the derivative is: $$\frac{dy}{dz} = 2z$$

For $$z=x^3$$, the derivative is: $$\frac{dz}{dx} = 3x^2$$

Step 2: Apply the Chain Rule formula.

$$ \frac{dy}{dx} = \frac{dy}{dz} \times \frac{dz}{dx} = (2z) \times (3x^2) $$

Step 3: Substitute z back in terms of x to get the final answer.

$$ \frac{dy}{dx} = 2(x^3) \times (3x^2) = 6x^5 $$

Both methods yield the same result, confirming the validity of the Chain Rule.
