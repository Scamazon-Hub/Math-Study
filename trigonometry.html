Trigonometry

1. Fundamentals of Right-Angled Triangles

When to Use

Use for any right-angled triangle to relate angles and side lengths.

Pythagoras' Theorem: For finding a third side when you know two sides.

$$ a^2 + b^2 = c^2 $$

SOH CAH TOA: For finding unknown sides or angles.

$$ \sin\theta = \frac{\text{Opposite}}{\text{Hypotenuse}}, \quad \cos\theta = \frac{\text{Adjacent}}{\text{Hypotenuse}}, \quad \tan\theta = \frac{\text{Opposite}}{\text{Adjacent}} $$

2. Radians & Circular Motion

What is a Radian?

A radian is the angle at the center of a circle created by an arc that has the same length as the circle's radius. Using radians simplifies many formulas in physics and engineering. A full circle is $$2\pi$$ radians.

Angle Conversions

Critical Reminder

You must convert angles to **radians** before using them in formulas for arc length, sector area, or any angular motion calculations.

Degrees to Radians: $$ \text{Radians} = \text{Degrees} \times \frac{\pi}{180} $$

Radians to Degrees: $$ \text{Degrees} = \text{Radians} \times \frac{180}{\pi} $$

Arc Length and Sector Area

Arc Length (s): $$ s = r\theta $$ (where $$\theta$$ is in **radians**)

Sector Area (A): $$ A = \frac{1}{2}r^2\theta $$ (where $$\theta$$ is in **radians**)

Example: A circular sector has a radius of 8cm and an angle of 60°. Find its arc length and area.

1. Convert angle to radians: $$ \theta = 60^\circ \times \frac{\pi}{180} = \frac{\pi}{3} \text{ rad} $$

2. Calculate Arc Length: $$ s = r\theta = 8 \times \frac{\pi}{3} \approx 8.38 \text{ cm} $$

3. Calculate Sector Area: $$ A = \frac{1}{2}r^2\theta = \frac{1}{2}(8^2)\left(\frac{\pi}{3}\right) = \frac{32\pi}{3} \approx 33.51 \text{ cm}^2 $$

Angular Motion & Centripetal Force

Key Formulas

These formulas connect rotational (angular) motion to linear (tangential) motion for an object moving in a circle.

Linear vs. Angular Speed ($$\omega$$): $$ v = r\omega $$

Centripetal Acceleration ($$a_c$$): $$ a_c = \omega^2r = \frac{v^2}{r} $$

Centripetal Force ($$F_c$$): $$ F_c = m\omega^2r = \frac{mv^2}{r} $$

Example: A 0.5 kg ball is swung on a 1.2m rope, completing 2 full rotations every second. Find its angular speed, linear speed, and the centripetal force in the rope.

1. Find angular speed ($$\omega$$) in rad/s:
Two rotations is $$2 \times 2\pi = 4\pi$$ radians. This occurs in 1 second.
$$ \omega = 4\pi \text{ rad/s} \approx 12.57 \text{ rad/s} $$

2. Find linear speed (v):
$$ v = r\omega = 1.2 \times 4\pi \approx 15.08 \text{ m/s} $$

3. Find centripetal force ($$F_c$$):
$$ F_c = \frac{mv^2}{r} = \frac{0.5 \times (15.08)^2}{1.2} \approx \frac{113.7}{1.2} \approx 94.75 \text{ N} $$

3. Rules for Non-Right-Angled Triangles

When to Use

Use for any triangle. Use the Sine Rule when you know an angle and its opposite side. Use the Cosine Rule when you know two sides and the included angle, or all three sides.

Sine Rule: $$ \frac{a}{\sin A} = \frac{b}{\sin B} = \frac{c}{\sin C} $$

Cosine Rule (for a side): $$ a^2 = b^2 + c^2 - 2bc\cos A $$

Cosine Rule (rearranged for an angle): $$ \cos A = \frac{b^2 + c^2 - a^2}{2bc} $$

Example: In a triangle, side b=10, side c=12, and angle A=48°. Find the length of side a.

We have two sides and the included angle, so we use the Cosine Rule:
$$ a^2 = 10^2 + 12^2 - 2(10)(12)\cos(48^\circ) $$
$$ a^2 = 100 + 144 - 240(0.6691) \approx 244 - 160.59 = 83.41 $$
$$ a = \sqrt{83.41} \approx 9.13 $$

4. Trigonometric Identities

When to Use

Use to expand, simplify, or rewrite trigonometric expressions involving the sum, difference, or multiples of angles. These are fundamental for solving complex trig equations.

Compound Angle (Sum & Difference) Formulas

$$ \sin(A \pm B) = \sin A \cos B \pm \cos A \sin B $$

$$ \cos(A \pm B) = \cos A \cos B \mp \sin A \sin B $$

Example: Find the exact value of $$ \sin(75^\circ) $$ without a calculator.

$$ \sin(75^\circ) = \sin(45^\circ+30^\circ) = \sin(45^\circ)\cos(30^\circ) + \cos(45^\circ)\sin(30^\circ) $$ $$ = \left(\frac{\sqrt{2}}{2}\right)\left(\frac{\sqrt{3}}{2}\right) + \left(\frac{\sqrt{2}}{2}\right)\left(\frac{1}{2}\right) = \frac{\sqrt{6}+\sqrt{2}}{4} $$

Double Angle Formulas

$$ \sin(2A) = 2\sin A \cos A $$

$$ \cos(2A) = \cos^2 A - \sin^2 A = 2\cos^2 A - 1 = 1 - 2\sin^2 A $$

Example: If $$ \sin A = \frac{4}{5} $$ and A is an acute angle, find the exact value of $$ \cos(2A) $$.

1. Find $$\cos A$$: We can imagine a right-angled triangle with opposite=4, hypotenuse=5. The adjacent side is $$ \sqrt{5^2 - 4^2} = \sqrt{9} = 3 $$. So, $$ \cos A = \frac{3}{5} $$.

2. Use the double angle formula:
$$ \cos(2A) = \cos^2 A - \sin^2 A = \left(\frac{3}{5}\right)^2 - \left(\frac{4}{5}\right)^2 = \frac{9}{25} - \frac{16}{25} = -\frac{7}{25} $$

5. Harmonic Form (R-alpha Method)

When to Use

Use to combine multiple sine and cosine waves of the same frequency into a single, phase-shifted wave. This is essential for finding the maximum amplitude and phase shift of a combined signal, e.g., in AC circuits or mechanical vibrations.

Basic Combination

To express $$ a\sin x + b\cos x $$ in the form $$ R\sin(x + \alpha) $$:

$$ R = \sqrt{a^2 + b^2} \quad \text{and} \quad \tan \alpha = \frac{b}{a} $$

Example: Given $$V_1 = 9\sin(\omega t)$$ and $$V_2 = 8\cos(\omega t)$$, express $$V_3 = V_1 + V_2$$ in the form $$R\sin(\omega t + \alpha)$$.

1. Identify 'a' and 'b': Here, $$a=9$$ and $$b=8$$.

2. Calculate the resultant amplitude (R):
$$ R = \sqrt{9^2 + 8^2} = \sqrt{81 + 64} = \sqrt{145} \approx 12.04 $$

3. Calculate the phase angle ($$\alpha$$) in radians:
$$ \tan \alpha = \frac{b}{a} = \frac{8}{9} \implies \alpha = \tan^{-1}\left(\frac{8}{9}\right) \approx 0.73 \text{ rad} $$

Final Answer: $$ V_3 \approx 12.04\sin(\omega t + 0.73) $$

Combining Waves with Existing Phase Shifts

Strategy

When combining waves that already have phase shifts, first use the compound angle formulas to expand each term. Then, collect all the $$\sin\theta$$ and $$\cos\theta$$ terms together. Finally, apply the standard R-alpha method to the simplified expression.

Example: Solve $$1.3\cos\theta + 0.8\sin(\theta + \pi/4) = 0.5$$ for its general solution in radians.

1. Expand the term with the phase shift:
$$ 0.8\sin(\theta + \pi/4) = 0.8(\sin\theta\cos(\pi/4) + \cos\theta\sin(\pi/4)) \approx 0.5657\sin\theta + 0.5657\cos\theta $$

2. Add to the original expression and collect terms:
$$ 1.3\cos\theta + (0.5657\sin\theta + 0.5657\cos\theta) = 0.5657\sin\theta + 1.8657\cos\theta $$

3. Convert to the form $$R\cos(\theta - \alpha)$$: (Using cos form as the cos term is larger)
$$ R = \sqrt{0.5657^2 + 1.8657^2} \approx 1.9496 $$ $$ \alpha = \arctan\left(\frac{0.5657}{1.8657}\right) \approx 0.2944 \text{ rad} $$ So the LHS becomes $$ 1.9496\cos(\theta - 0.2944) $$

4. Solve the simplified equation:
$$ 1.9496\cos(\theta - 0.2944) = 0.5 \implies \cos(\theta - 0.2944) \approx 0.2565 $$

5. Find all solutions for the angle:
The principal value is $$\beta = \arccos(0.2565) \approx 1.3114 \text{ rad}$$.
Due to the symmetry of the cosine graph, the other solution in the range $$[0, 2\pi]$$ is $$2\pi - 1.3114 = 4.9718 \text{ rad}$$.

6. Solve for $$\theta$$ and write the general solution ($$n$$ is any integer):
$$ \theta - 0.2944 = 1.3114 \implies \theta \approx 1.6058 $$.
$$ \theta - 0.2944 = 4.9718 \implies \theta \approx 5.2662 $$.

Final Answer: $$ \theta \approx 1.6058 + 2\pi n $$ and $$ \theta \approx 5.2662 + 2\pi n $$.

6. Solving Quadratic Trigonometric Equations

When to Use

For equations containing a squared trig function (like $$\sin^2x$$) that can be factorised or solved using the quadratic formula. You may need to use identities like $$\sin^2x + \cos^2x = 1$$ to get the equation in terms of a single trig function first.

Example: Solve $$(2\sin x - 1)(4\sin x + 1) = 0$$ for $$0^\circ \le x \le 360^\circ$$.

1. Set each factor to zero:
$$ 2\sin x - 1 = 0 \implies \sin x = 0.5 $$ or $$ 4\sin x + 1 = 0 \implies \sin x = -0.25 $$

2. Solve for each case within the range $$0^\circ \le x \le 360^\circ$$:
For $$\sin x = 0.5$$, the principal value is $$x = \sin^{-1}(0.5) = 30^\circ$$. The other solution is $$180^\circ - 30^\circ = 150^\circ$$.
For $$\sin x = -0.25$$, the principal value is $$x = \sin^{-1}(-0.25) \approx -14.48^\circ$$. This is outside the range. The solutions inside the range are $$180^\circ - (-14.48^\circ) = 194.48^\circ$$ and $$360^\circ + (-14.48^\circ) = 345.52^\circ$$.

Final Answer: The solutions are $$30^\circ, 150^\circ, 194.48^\circ, \text{ and } 345.52^\circ$$.
