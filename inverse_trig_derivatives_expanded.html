Inverse Trig Derivatives: Expanded Workings

This section provides a more detailed look at the derivatives of inverse trigonometric functions. [cite_start]We will show the full, step-by-step derivations for $$\arccos(x)$$ and $$\arctan(x)$$, which were left as exercises previously. [cite: 12] We will also explore how to combine these rules with the chain rule for more complex functions.

Deriving the Derivative of arccos(x)

Problem: Find the derivative of $$y = \arccos(x)$$.

Step 1: Rewrite the function in terms of x

If $$y = \arccos(x)$$, we can express $$x$$ as a function of $$y$$:

$$x = \cos(y)$$

Step 2: Differentiate with respect to y

Find the derivative of $$x$$ with respect to $$y$$. The derivative of $$\cos(y)$$ is $$-\sin(y)$$.

$$ \frac{dx}{dy} = -\sin(y) $$

Step 3: Use a trigonometric identity to express $$\sin(y)$$ in terms of x

[cite_start]

From the identity $$\sin^2(y) + \cos^2(y) = 1$$, we get $$\sin(y) = \sqrt{1 - \cos^2(y)}$$. [cite: 7] Since $$x = \cos(y)$$, we can substitute this in:

$$ \sin(y) = \sqrt{1 - x^2} $$

Therefore, $$\frac{dx}{dy} = -\sqrt{1-x^2}$$.

Step 4: Find the reciprocal to get $$\frac{dy}{dx}$$

[cite_start]

The derivative we want, $$\frac{dy}{dx}$$, is the reciprocal of $$\frac{dx}{dy}$$. [cite: 11]

$$ \frac{dy}{dx} = \frac{1}{\frac{dx}{dy}} = \frac{1}{-\sqrt{1-x^2}} = \frac{-1}{\sqrt{1-x^2}} $$
[cite_start]

Conclusion: This confirms the formula for the derivative of $$\arccos(x)$$. [cite: 14]

Deriving the Derivative of arctan(x)

Problem: Find the derivative of $$y = \arctan(x)$$.

Step 1: Rewrite the function in terms of x

$$y = \arctan(x) \implies x = \tan(y)$$

Step 2: Differentiate with respect to y

The derivative of $$\tan(y)$$ is $$\sec^2(y)$$.

$$ \frac{dx}{dy} = \sec^2(y) $$

Step 3: Use a trigonometric identity to express $$\sec^2(y)$$ in terms of x

[cite_start]

We use the identity $$1 + \tan^2(y) = \sec^2(y)$$. [cite: 13] Since $$x = \tan(y)$$, we can substitute this in:

$$ \sec^2(y) = 1 + x^2 $$

Therefore, $$\frac{dx}{dy} = 1+x^2$$.

Step 4: Find the reciprocal to get $$\frac{dy}{dx}$$

$$ \frac{dy}{dx} = \frac{1}{\frac{dx}{dy}} = \frac{1}{1+x^2} $$
[cite_start]

Conclusion: This confirms the formula for the derivative of $$\arctan(x)$$. [cite: 14]

Applying the Rules with the Chain Rule

When the input to the inverse trigonometric function is another function of $$x$$ (e.g., $$\arcsin(u)$$ where $$u=f(x)$$), we must apply the chain rule.

Inverse Trig Derivatives with the Chain Rule

If $$u$$ is a function of $$x$$:

  • $$\frac{d}{dx}(\arcsin u) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{1-u^2}} \cdot \frac{du}{dx}$$
  • $$\frac{d}{dx}(\arccos u) = \frac{-1}{\sqrt{1-u^2}} \cdot \frac{du}{dx}$$
  • $$\frac{d}{dx}(\arctan u) = \frac{1}{1+u^2} \cdot \frac{du}{dx}$$

Example: Differentiate $$y = \arcsin(5x)$$

Here, the inner function is $$u = 5x$$, so $$\frac{du}{dx} = 5$$.

Using the chain rule formula for $$\arcsin(u)$$, we get:

$$ \frac{dy}{dx} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{1-(5x)^2}} \cdot 5 $$

Final Answer:

$$ \frac{dy}{dx} = \frac{5}{\sqrt{1-25x^2}} $$

Example: Differentiate $$y = \arctan(x^2)$$

The inner function is $$u = x^2$$, so $$\frac{du}{dx} = 2x$$.

Using the chain rule formula for $$\arctan(u)$$, we get:

$$ \frac{dy}{dx} = \frac{1}{1+(x^2)^2} \cdot 2x $$

Final Answer:

$$ \frac{dy}{dx} = \frac{2x}{1+x^4} $$
