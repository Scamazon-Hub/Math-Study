The Quotient Rule

The rule for the differentiation of quotients has a similar form to the 'product rule', and again the emphasis is placed on the application of the rule rather than its derivation. For this reason the derivation is offered as optional reading in the Appendix.

The Quotient Rule

If $$u$$ and $$v$$ are functions of $$x$$ such that $$f(x) = \frac{u}{v}$$, then:

$$ f'(x) = \frac{v\frac{du}{dx} - u\frac{dv}{dx}}{v^2} $$

Comparing with the 'product rule' we see two differences: the plus sign has become a minus sign and the $$v^2$$ divisor has been introduced.

We can still make use of the 'cross-product' layout which we employed for the product rule, but the presence of the minus sign adds significance to the sequence of terms.

Example 1 illustrates this by numbering the two cross-products.

Example 1

Problem: Obtain $$\frac{dy}{dx}$$ for the quotient $$y = \frac{x^2}{x-1}$$

Step 1: Assign u and v

Let $$u = x^2$$ and $$v = x-1$$

Step 2: Differentiate u and v

Differentiating each function with respect to $$x$$ gives:

  • $$\frac{du}{dx} = 2x$$
  • $$\frac{dv}{dx} = 1$$

Step 3: Apply the quotient rule formula

Apply the 'quotient rule' using the cross products. The term $$v\frac{du}{dx}$$ must come first.

$$ \frac{dy}{dx} = \frac{v\frac{du}{dx} - u\frac{dv}{dx}}{v^2} $$

Substitute the functions and their derivatives into the formula:

$$ \frac{dy}{dx} = \frac{(x-1)(2x) - (x^2)(1)}{(x-1)^2} $$

Now, simplify the numerator:

$$ \frac{dy}{dx} = \frac{2x^2 - 2x - x^2}{(x-1)^2} $$

Final Answer:

$$ \frac{dy}{dx} = \frac{x^2 - 2x}{(x-1)^2} $$
