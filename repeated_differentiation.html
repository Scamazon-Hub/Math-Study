Higher-Order & Repeated Differentiation

The process of differentiation can be applied repeatedly. Taking the derivative of a function's derivative is called finding the second derivative, and continuing the process yields higher-order derivatives. These derivatives provide deeper insight into a function's behavior, particularly its shape, curvature, and rates of change.

The gradient at a point on a curve changes as the point moves along the curve. The rate of this change is determined by the curve's curvature. This 'rate of change of the gradient' is what the second derivative represents.

Notation for Higher-Order Derivatives

The rate of change of the gradient, $$\frac{d}{dx}\left(\frac{dy}{dx}\right)$$, is denoted as follows. Higher-order derivatives follow a similar pattern.

Order Function Notation Leibniz Notation
First Derivative $$f'(x)$$ $$\frac{dy}{dx}$$
Second Derivative $$f''(x)$$ $$\frac{d^2y}{dx^2}$$
Third Derivative $$f'''(x)$$ $$\frac{d^3y}{dx^3}$$
n-th Derivative $$f^{(n)}(x)$$ $$\frac{d^ny}{dx^n}$$

Geometric Interpretation: Concavity

The second derivative tells us about the shape or curvature of a function's graph. This is a crucial tool for curve sketching and analysis.

  • If $$f''(x) > 0$$ on an interval, the graph is concave up (shaped like a cup). This means the slope, $$f'(x)$$, is increasing.
  • If $$f''(x) < 0$$ on an interval, the graph is concave down (shaped like a frown). This means the slope, $$f'(x)$$, is decreasing.
  • A point of inflection is a point on the graph where the concavity changes. This typically occurs where $$f''(x) = 0$$.

Example 1: Finding Concavity and Inflection Points

Problem: For the function $$f(x) = x^3 - 6x^2 + 5$$, find the intervals of concavity and any points of inflection.

Step 1: Find the first and second derivatives

$$ f'(x) = 3x^2 - 12x $$
$$ f''(x) = 6x - 12 $$

Step 2: Find potential points of inflection

Set the second derivative to zero and solve for $$x$$.

$$ 6x - 12 = 0 \implies x = 2 $$

Step 3: Test the intervals around $$x=2$$

  • For $$x < 2$$ (e.g., $$x=0$$): $$f''(0) = -12$$. Since $$f''(x) < 0$$, the graph is concave down.
  • For $$x > 2$$ (e.g., $$x=3$$): $$f''(3) = 6$$. Since $$f''(x) > 0$$, the graph is concave up.

Conclusion: The function is concave down on $$(-\infty, 2)$$ and concave up on $$(2, \infty)$$. Since concavity changes at $$x=2$$, there is a point of inflection at $$x=2$$. The y-coordinate is $$f(2) = (2)^3 - 6(2)^2 + 5 = -11$$. The point of inflection is at $$(2, -11)$$.

Physical Interpretation: Linear Motion

One of the most intuitive applications of higher-order derivatives is in mechanics, relating displacement, velocity, and acceleration.

Displacement, Velocity, and Acceleration

  • Displacement: $$s = f(t)$$
  • Velocity (First Derivative): $$v = \frac{ds}{dt} = f'(t)$$
  • Acceleration (Second Derivative): $$a = \frac{dv}{dt} = \frac{d^2s}{dt^2} = f''(t)$$

Example 2: Analyzing Linear Motion

Problem: The motion of a body is described by $$s = 2t^3 - 8.5t^2 + 12t - 8$$, where s is in metres and t is in seconds.

a. Find the velocity at t = 2s

Workings: First, find the velocity function, $$v(t)$$, by differentiating $$s(t)$$.

$$ v(t) = \frac{ds}{dt} = 6t^2 - 17t + 12 $$

Now substitute $$t=2$$:

$$ v(2) = 6(2)^2 - 17(2) + 12 = 24 - 34 + 12 = 2 $$

Answer: The velocity at t=2s is $$2 \text{ m/s}$$.

b. Find the acceleration at t = 3s

Workings: Find the acceleration function, $$a(t)$$, by differentiating $$v(t)$$.

$$ a(t) = \frac{dv}{dt} = \frac{d^2s}{dt^2} = 12t - 17 $$

Now substitute $$t=3$$:

$$ a(3) = 12(3) - 17 = 36 - 17 = 19 $$

Answer: The acceleration at t=3s is $$19 \text{ m/s}^2$$.

c. Find when the body is stationary

Workings: The body is stationary when velocity is zero. Set $$v(t) = 0$$ and solve.

$$ 6t^2 - 17t + 12 = 0 $$

Factor the quadratic: $$(2t - 3)(3t - 4) = 0$$. This gives two solutions: $$t = \frac{3}{2}$$ and $$t = \frac{4}{3}$$.

Answer: The body is stationary at $$t = 1.33$$s and $$t = 1.5$$s.

Advanced Example: Repeated Product Rule

Problem: Given $$y = e^{3x}\sin(x)$$, show that $$\frac{d^2y}{dx^2} - 6\frac{dy}{dx} + 10y = 0$$.

Step 1: Find the first derivative, $$\frac{dy}{dx}$$

Use the product rule with $$u=e^{3x}$$ and $$v=\sin(x)$$.

$$ \frac{dy}{dx} = (3e^{3x})(\sin x) + (e^{3x})(\cos x) = e^{3x}(3\sin x + \cos x) $$

Step 2: Find the second derivative, $$\frac{d^2y}{dx^2}$$

Apply the product rule again to $$\frac{dy}{dx}$$. Let $$u=e^{3x}$$ and $$v=3\sin x + \cos x$$.

$$ \frac{d^2y}{dx^2} = (3e^{3x})(3\sin x + \cos x) + (e^{3x})(3\cos x - \sin x) $$

Simplify by expanding and collecting terms:

$$ \frac{d^2y}{dx^2} = 9e^{3x}\sin x + 3e^{3x}\cos x + 3e^{3x}\cos x - e^{3x}\sin x $$
$$ \frac{d^2y}{dx^2} = 8e^{3x}\sin x + 6e^{3x}\cos x $$

Step 3: Substitute into the differential equation

Substitute the expressions for $$y$$, $$\frac{dy}{dx}$$, and $$\frac{d^2y}{dx^2}$$ into the target equation:

$$(8e^{3x}\sin x + 6e^{3x}\cos x) - 6(e^{3x}(3\sin x + \cos x)) + 10(e^{3x}\sin x) = 0$$

Expand and simplify:

$$ 8e^{3x}\sin x + 6e^{3x}\cos x - 18e^{3x}\sin x - 6e^{3x}\cos x + 10e^{3x}\sin x = 0 $$

Combine like terms: $$ (8 - 18 + 10)e^{3x}\sin x + (6 - 6)e^{3x}\cos x = 0 $$

$$ 0 \cdot e^{3x}\sin x + 0 \cdot e^{3x}\cos x = 0 $$

Conclusion: Since $$0=0$$, the original equation is shown to be true.
