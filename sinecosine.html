Calculus of Sine & Cosine Functions

1. Differentiation of Sine and Cosine

When to Use

These rules are fundamental for finding the rate of change in systems described by sinusoidal waveforms, such as AC circuits, mechanical vibrations, and wave mechanics. A critical point is that these rules are valid when the angle (x) is measured in radians.

General Differentiation Formulas

Function ($$y$$)Derivative ($$\frac{dy}{dx}$$)
$$y = a \sin(kx)$$$$\frac{dy}{dx} = ak \cos(kx)$$
$$y = a \cos(kx)$$$$\frac{dy}{dx} = -ak \sin(kx)$$

2. Graphical Interpretation

The Gradient Connection

The derivative of a function gives its gradient. For trigonometric functions, this means:

  • The gradient of the $$y = \sin(x)$$ curve at any point is numerically equal to $$\cos(x)$$.
  • The gradient of the $$y = \cos(x)$$ curve at any point is numerically equal to $$-\sin(x)$$.

For the sine wave above, at $$x=1.5\pi$$, the tangent is horizontal, so its gradient is 0. This matches the rule, as $$\cos(1.5\pi) = 0$$.

For the cosine wave, at $$x=\pi/6$$, the gradient of the tangent is -0.5. This matches the rule, as $$-\sin(\pi/6) = -0.5$$.

3. Worked Examples

Example 1: Rate of Change of Voltage

A voltage is given by the expression $$v = 200\sin\theta$$ volts. Evaluate the rate of change, $$\frac{dv}{d\theta}$$, when $$\theta = 0.86$$ radians.

Step 1: Differentiate the expression.

$$ \frac{dv}{d\theta} = 200\cos\theta $$

Step 2: Substitute the value for $$\theta$$. Make sure your calculator is in RADIAN mode.

$$ \left.\frac{dv}{d\theta}\right|_{\theta=0.86} = 200\cos(0.86) \approx 130.5 $$

The rate of change is 130.5 V/rad.

Example 2: Gradient of a Curve

Evaluate the gradient of the curve $$y = 100\cos\theta$$ at the point where $$\theta = 5.2$$ radians.

Step 1: Differentiate the expression.

$$ \frac{dy}{d\theta} = 100(-\sin\theta) = -100\sin\theta $$

Step 2: Substitute the value for $$\theta$$.

$$ \left.\frac{dy}{d\theta}\right|_{\theta=5.2} = -100\sin(5.2) = -100(-0.88345) \approx 88.3 $$

The gradient of the curve at that point is approximately 88.3.

Example 3: Rate of Change of Torque

A variable torque is given by $$T = 2000\sin\theta + 3000\cos\theta$$. Determine the rate of change of torque with respect to angular position, $$\frac{dT}{d\theta}$$, when $$\theta = \pi/4$$ radians.

Step 1: Differentiate the expression term by term.

$$ \frac{dT}{d\theta} = 2000\cos\theta - 3000\sin\theta $$

Step 2: Substitute the value for $$\theta$$.

$$ \left.\frac{dT}{d\theta}\right|_{\theta=\pi/4} = 2000\cos(\pi/4) - 3000\sin(\pi/4) $$ $$ = 2000\left(\frac{\sqrt{2}}{2}\right) - 3000\left(\frac{\sqrt{2}}{2}\right) = 1414.2 - 2121.3 \approx -707.1 $$

The rate of change of torque is -707.1 Nm/rad.
