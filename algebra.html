Core Algebra

1. The Quadratic Equation

When to Use

Use to find the roots (solutions) of any quadratic equation in the form $$ax^2 + bx + c = 0$$.

$$ x = \frac{-b \pm \sqrt{b^2-4ac}}{2a} $$

Example: Solve $$ 2x^2 + 5x - 3 = 0 $$

Step 1: Identify coefficients. $$a=2, b=5, c=-3$$

Step 2: Substitute into the formula. $$ x = \frac{-(5) \pm \sqrt{(5)^2 - 4(2)(-3)}}{2(2)} $$

Step 3: Solve. $$ x = \frac{-5 \pm \sqrt{25 + 24}}{4} = \frac{-5 \pm \sqrt{49}}{4} = \frac{-5 \pm 7}{4} $$

Step 4: Find the two roots. $$ x_1 = \frac{-5 + 7}{4} = 0.5 $$ and $$ x_2 = \frac{-5 - 7}{4} = -3 $$

2. Polynomials

Remainder & Factor Theorems

These theorems provide a quick way to find the remainder of a polynomial division and to find factors.

  • Remainder Theorem: If a polynomial $$f(x)$$ is divided by $$(x-a)$$, the remainder is $$f(a)$$.
  • Factor Theorem: $$(x-a)$$ is a factor of $$f(x)$$ if and only if $$f(a) = 0$$.

Example: Given $$f(x) = x^3 + 2x^2 - 5x - 6$$, show that $$(x-2)$$ is a factor.

Using the Factor Theorem, we test $$f(2)$$:
$$ f(2) = (2)^3 + 2(2)^2 - 5(2) - 6 = 8 + 8 - 10 - 6 = 0 $$

Since $$f(2) = 0$$, $$(x-2)$$ is a factor of $$f(x)$$.

Polynomial Division

When to Use

Use to divide a polynomial by another of a lower degree. This is essential for simplifying rational expressions and finding all factors of a polynomial.

Example: Divide $$ x^3 + 2x^2 - 5x - 6 $$ by $$ x - 2 $$

The division process yields a quotient of $$ x^2 + 4x + 3 $$ with a remainder of 0, confirming the result from the Factor Theorem.

This means we can write $$ x^3 + 2x^2 - 5x - 6 = (x-2)(x^2+4x+3) $$. By factoring the quadratic part, we get the fully factored form: $$ (x-2)(x+1)(x+3) $$.

3. Arithmetic & Geometric Progression

When to Use

For sequences of numbers with a constant difference (Arithmetic) or a constant ratio (Geometric).

Arithmetic Progression (AP)

nth term: $$ u_n = a + (n-1)d $$

Sum of n terms: $$ S_n = \frac{n}{2}[2a + (n-1)d] $$

Geometric Progression (GP)

nth term: $$ u_n = ar^{n-1} $$

Sum of n terms: $$ S_n = \frac{a(1-r^n)}{1-r} $$

Sum to Infinity: $$ S_\infty = \frac{a}{1-r} $$, (for $$-1 < r < 1$$).

4. Complex Numbers

When to Use

Essential for analyzing AC electrical circuits, control theory, and signal processing, where phase and amplitude must be considered together.

A complex number $$z$$ is a number of the form $$z = a + bi$$, where 'a' is the real part, 'b' is the imaginary part, and $$i = \sqrt{-1}$$. They can be visualized on an **Argand diagram**, with a horizontal real axis and a vertical imaginary axis.

Basic Operations

Let $$z_1 = 3+4i$$ and $$z_2 = 5-2i$$.

  • Addition/Subtraction: $$ z_1+z_2 = (3+5) + (4-2)i = 8+2i $$
  • Multiplication: $$ z_1z_2 = (3+4i)(5-2i) = 15 - 6i + 20i - 8i^2 = 15 + 14i - 8(-1) = 23+14i $$
  • Division: Multiply the top and bottom by the complex conjugate of the denominator ($$\bar{z_2} = 5+2i$$).
    $$ \frac{z_1}{z_2} = \frac{3+4i}{5-2i} \times \frac{5+2i}{5+2i} = \frac{15+6i+20i+8i^2}{25-4i^2} = \frac{7+26i}{29} = \frac{7}{29} + \frac{26}{29}i $$

Forms of Complex Numbers

A complex number can be expressed in three main ways:

  • Rectangular Form: $$ z = a+bi $$
  • Polar Form: $$ z = r(\cos\theta + i\sin\theta) = r\angle\theta $$
  • Exponential Form: $$ z = re^{i\theta} $$ (based on Euler's formula)

Example: Convert $$z = 4 + 3i$$ to Polar and Exponential Forms.

1. Find the modulus (r): $$ r = \sqrt{4^2 + 3^2} = \sqrt{16+9} = 5 $$

2. Find the argument ($$\theta$$): $$ \theta = \tan^{-1}\left(\frac{3}{4}\right) \approx 36.87^\circ $$ or $$0.6435$$ radians.

Polar Form: $$ z = 5\angle36.87^\circ $$

Exponential Form: $$ z = 5e^{i0.6435} $$
