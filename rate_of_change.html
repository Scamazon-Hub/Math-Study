Differentiation as a Rate of Change

1. The Fundamental Interpretation

What is a Derivative?

Any introduction to differentiation stresses the **rate of change** interpretation. In fact, differentiation can be described as: "an algebraic process for the derivation of rate of change expressions".

Graphically, the 'rate of change' is the **gradient of the tangent to a curve** at a specific point. This allows many derivatives to have a direct physical interpretation.

2. Physical Interpretations & Examples

Applications in Science and Engineering

Many physical laws are defined in terms of rates of change, which makes differentiation a critical tool for analysis.

If $$s$$ represents the linear displacement of a moving body from a fixed reference point, and $$t$$ represents time, we can express the relationship between them as a function:

$$ s = f(t) $$

Velocity

Velocity is defined as the 'rate of change of displacement'. Therefore, it is the derivative of displacement with respect to time.

$$ v = \frac{ds}{dt} = f'(t) $$

Other Rates of Change

This concept applies to any changing quantity. For example:

The rate of change of temperature, $$\theta$$, with respect to time:

$$ \frac{d\theta}{dt} \quad \text{or} \quad f'(t) $$

The rate of change of pressure, $$p$$, with respect to distance $$x$$ along a hydraulic pipeline:

$$ \frac{dp}{dx} \quad \text{or} \quad f'(x) $$

3. The Second Derivative

Rate of Change of the Rate of Change

The **second derivative** describes how the gradient (the first rate of change) is itself changing. This tells us about the **curvature** of the graph. It is written as $$\frac{d^2y}{dx^2}$$ or $$f''(x)$$.

Physical Meaning: Displacement, Velocity, and Acceleration

If we apply two stages of differentiation to a displacement expression, we obtain an expression for acceleration.

  • Displacement: $$ s = f(t) $$
  • Velocity is the rate of change of displacement: $$ v = \frac{ds}{dt} = f'(t) $$
  • Acceleration is the rate of change of velocity: $$ a = \frac{dv}{dt} = \frac{d^2s}{dt^2} = f''(t) $$

Example: Evaluate $$f''(2)$$ for the function $$f(x) = 2x^3 - x^2 + 3x + 1$$.

Step 1: Differentiate to find the first derivative, $$f'(x)$$.

$$ f'(x) = 6x^2 - 2x + 3 $$

Step 2: Differentiate again to find the second derivative, $$f''(x)$$.

$$ f''(x) = 12x - 2 $$

Step 3: Substitute x=2 into the second derivative.

$$ f''(2) = 12(2) - 2 $$

Step 4: Calculate the final value.

$$ f''(2) = 24 - 2 = 22 $$
