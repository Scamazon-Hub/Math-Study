Calculus

1. Differentiation

What is it?

Differentiation is the process of finding the derivative, which represents the instantaneous rate of change or the gradient of a function at a specific point.

Standard Derivatives

Function ($$y$$) Derivative ($$\frac{dy}{dx}$$)
$$x^n$$$$nx^{n-1}$$
$$e^{ax}$$$$ae^{ax}$$
$$\ln(x)$$$$\frac{1}{x}$$
$$\sin(ax)$$$$a\cos(ax)$$
$$\cos(ax)$$$$-a\sin(ax)$$
$$\tan(ax)$$$$a\sec^2(ax)$$

Differentiation Rules

Product Rule:

$$ \frac{d}{dx}(uv) = u\frac{dv}{dx} + v\frac{du}{dx} $$

Quotient Rule:

$$ \frac{d}{dx}\left(\frac{u}{v}\right) = \frac{v\frac{du}{dx} - u\frac{dv}{dx}}{v^2} $$

Chain Rule:

$$ \frac{dy}{dx} = \frac{dy}{du} \times \frac{du}{dx} $$

Example (Chain Rule): Differentiate $$ y = (4x-3)^5 $$

Step 1: Identify the inner function (u). Let $$u = 4x-3$$. Then $$y=u^5$$.

Step 2: Differentiate both parts. $$ \frac{du}{dx} = 4 $$ and $$ \frac{dy}{du} = 5u^4 $$.

Step 3: Apply the chain rule. $$ \frac{dy}{dx} = \frac{dy}{du} \times \frac{du}{dx} = 5u^4 \times 4 = 20u^4 $$

Final Answer: $$ \frac{dy}{dx} = 20(4x-3)^4 $$

Example (Quotient Rule): Differentiate $$ y = \frac{2x}{\sin(3x)} $$

Step 1: Identify u and v. Let $$u=2x$$ and $$v=\sin(3x)$$.

Step 2: Differentiate u and v. $$ \frac{du}{dx} = 2 $$ and $$ \frac{dv}{dx} = 3\cos(3x) $$.

Step 3: Apply the quotient rule.

$$ \frac{dy}{dx} = \frac{(\sin(3x))(2) - (2x)(3\cos(3x))}{(\sin(3x))^2} $$

Final Answer: $$ \frac{dy}{dx} = \frac{2\sin(3x) - 6x\cos(3x)}{\sin^2(3x)} $$

2. Integration

What is it?

Integration is the reverse process of differentiation. A definite integral is used to find the total accumulation of a quantity, such as the area under a curve.

Standard Integrals

Function ($$y$$) Integral ($$\int y \,dx$$)
$$x^n$$$$\frac{x^{n+1}}{n+1} + C$$
$$e^{ax}$$$$\frac{1}{a}e^{ax} + C$$
$$\frac{1}{x}$$$$\ln|x| + C$$
$$\sin(ax)$$$$-\frac{1}{a}\cos(ax) + C$$
$$\cos(ax)$$$$\frac{1}{a}\sin(ax) + C$$

Applications of Integration

Area under a curve: $$ A = \int_{a}^{b} y \, dx $$

Volume of Revolution (about x-axis): $$ V = \pi \int_{a}^{b} y^2 \, dx $$

Example (Area): Find the area under the curve $$y=x^2+1$$ from $$x=0$$ to $$x=2$$.

$$ A = \int_{0}^{2} (x^2+1) \, dx = \left[ \frac{x^3}{3} + x \right]_{0}^{2} $$ $$ A = \left( \frac{2^3}{3} + 2 \right) - (0) = \frac{8}{3} + 2 = \frac{14}{3} \approx 4.67 \text{ units}^2 $$

Example (Volume): Find the volume generated when the line $$y=2x$$ from $$x=0$$ to $$x=3$$ is rotated about the x-axis.

$$ V = \pi \int_{0}^{3} (2x)^2 \, dx = \pi \int_{0}^{3} 4x^2 \, dx $$ $$ V = \pi \left[ \frac{4x^3}{3} \right]_{0}^{3} = \pi \left( \frac{4(3)^3}{3} - 0 \right) = \pi(36) \approx 113.1 \text{ units}^3 $$

3. Stationary Points & Optimisation

When to Use

Use to find the maximum, minimum, or points of inflection of a function. This is crucial for optimization problems (e.g., finding maximum strength or minimum cost).

Stationary points occur when the gradient is zero: $$ \frac{dy}{dx} = 0 $$.

To determine the nature of a stationary point, use the Second Derivative Test:

  • If $$ \frac{d^2y}{dx^2} > 0 $$, the point is a local minimum.
  • If $$ \frac{d^2y}{dx^2} < 0 $$, the point is a local maximum.
  • If $$ \frac{d^2y}{dx^2} = 0 $$, the test is inconclusive.

Example: Find and classify the stationary points of $$y = x^3 - 12x + 2$$

1. Find the first derivative: $$ \frac{dy}{dx} = 3x^2 - 12 $$

2. Find stationary points (set to zero): $$ 3x^2 - 12 = 0 \implies x^2 = 4 \implies x = \pm 2 $$

3. Find the second derivative: $$ \frac{d^2y}{dx^2} = 6x $$

4. Test each x-value:
At $$x=2$$: $$ \frac{d^2y}{dx^2} = 6(2) = 12 $$. Since $$12 > 0$$, this is a local minimum.
At $$x=-2$$: $$ \frac{d^2y}{dx^2} = 6(-2) = -12 $$. Since $$-12 < 0$$, this is a local maximum.

4. Hyperbolic Functions

When to Use

These functions appear in specific engineering problems, such as the shape of a hanging cable (a catenary curve), described by the hyperbolic cosine function (cosh).

Definitions: $$ \sinh x = \frac{e^x - e^{-x}}{2} \quad | \quad \cosh x = \frac{e^x + e^{-x}}{2} $$

Key Identity: $$ \cosh^2 x - \sinh^2 x = 1 $$

Derivatives & Integrals:

  • $$ \frac{d}{dx}(\sinh x) = \cosh x $$
  • $$ \frac{d}{dx}(\cosh x) = \sinh x $$
  • $$ \int \sinh x \,dx = \cosh x + C $$
  • $$ \int \cosh x \,dx = \sinh x + C $$
