Vectors

Vector Fundamentals

Scalars vs. Vectors

  • A Scalar is a quantity that has magnitude (size) only. Examples: speed, distance, mass, time.
  • A Vector is a quantity that has both magnitude and direction. Examples: velocity, force, displacement.

Scalar Multiplication

Definition

Multiplying a vector by a scalar changes its magnitude. If the scalar is negative, it also reverses the vector's direction. The vector remains parallel to its original line of action.

Unit Vectors (i, j, k)

A unit vector has a magnitude of 1 and is used to specify a direction. The special unit vectors $$\mathbf{i}$$, $$\mathbf{j}$$, and $$\mathbf{k}$$ represent the directions of the x, y, and z axes in a Cartesian coordinate system.

Magnitude of a Vector

Definition

The magnitude (or length) of a vector $$\mathbf{v} = x\mathbf{i} + y\mathbf{j} + z\mathbf{k}$$ is calculated using the Pythagorean theorem in 3D:

$$ |\mathbf{v}| = \sqrt{x^2 + y^2 + z^2} $$

In mechanical engineering, this is crucial for determining the strength of forces or velocities.

Example: Find the magnitude of the vector $$\mathbf{v} = 3\mathbf{i} - 4\mathbf{j} + 5\mathbf{k}$$

$$ |\mathbf{v}| = \sqrt{3^2 + (-4)^2 + 5^2} = \sqrt{9 + 16 + 25} = \sqrt{50} \approx 7.07 $$

Calculating Unit Vectors

Definition

A unit vector $$\hat{\mathbf{v}}$$ in the direction of $$\mathbf{v}$$ is obtained by dividing the vector by its magnitude:

$$ \hat{\mathbf{v}} = \frac{\mathbf{v}}{|\mathbf{v}|} $$

This is useful in engineering for normalizing forces or directions in simulations.

Example: Find the unit vector for $$\mathbf{v} = 3\mathbf{i} - 4\mathbf{j} + 5\mathbf{k}$$

$$ \hat{\mathbf{v}} = \frac{3\mathbf{i} - 4\mathbf{j} + 5\mathbf{k}}{\sqrt{50}} = \frac{3}{\sqrt{50}}\mathbf{i} - \frac{4}{\sqrt{50}}\mathbf{j} + \frac{5}{\sqrt{50}}\mathbf{k} \approx 0.424\mathbf{i} - 0.566\mathbf{j} + 0.707\mathbf{k} $$

Vector Addition and Subtraction

Triangle Rule

How it Works

To add two vectors $$\mathbf{a}$$ and $$\mathbf{b}$$, place the start of vector $$\mathbf{b}$$ at the end of vector $$\mathbf{a}$$. The sum, or resultant vector, is obtained by the third line which completes the triangle.

Component Method for Addition

How it Works

For vectors $$\mathbf{a} = a_x\mathbf{i} + a_y\mathbf{j} + a_z\mathbf{k}$$ and $$\mathbf{b} = b_x\mathbf{i} + b_y\mathbf{j} + b_z\mathbf{k}$$, the sum is:

$$ \mathbf{a} + \mathbf{b} = (a_x + b_x)\mathbf{i} + (a_y + b_y)\mathbf{j} + (a_z + b_z)\mathbf{k} $$

This method is efficient for multiple vectors in mechanical systems, such as summing forces in a truss.

Example: Add $$\mathbf{a} = 2\mathbf{i} + 3\mathbf{j} - 1\mathbf{k}$$ and $$\mathbf{b} = 4\mathbf{i} - 2\mathbf{j} + 5\mathbf{k}$$

$$ \mathbf{a} + \mathbf{b} = (2+4)\mathbf{i} + (3-2)\mathbf{j} + (-1+5)\mathbf{k} = 6\mathbf{i} + 1\mathbf{j} + 4\mathbf{k} $$

Vector Subtraction

How it Works

Subtracting $$\mathbf{b}$$ from $$\mathbf{a}$$ is equivalent to adding $$\mathbf{a}$$ and $$-\mathbf{b}$$. Using components: $$\mathbf{a} - \mathbf{b} = (a_x - b_x)\mathbf{i} + (a_y - b_y)\mathbf{j} + (a_z - b_z)\mathbf{k}$$

In engineering, this is used to find relative velocities or displacements.

Example: Subtract $$\mathbf{b} = 4\mathbf{i} - 2\mathbf{j} + 5\mathbf{k}$$ from $$\mathbf{a} = 2\mathbf{i} + 3\mathbf{j} - 1\mathbf{k}$$

$$ \mathbf{a} - \mathbf{b} = (2-4)\mathbf{i} + (3+2)\mathbf{j} + (-1-5)\mathbf{k} = -2\mathbf{i} + 5\mathbf{j} - 6\mathbf{k} $$

Resolving Vectors into Components

When to Use

To analyze forces or velocities that don't act purely horizontally or vertically. Any vector can be broken down into two perpendicular components, which is essential for adding multiple vectors together by components.

For a vector $$\mathbf{F}$$ with magnitude $$|\mathbf{F}|$$ at an angle $$\theta$$ to the horizontal:

Horizontal Component ($$F_1$$):

$$ F_1 = |\mathbf{F}|\cos\theta $$

Vertical Component ($$F_2$$):

$$ F_2 = |\mathbf{F}|\sin\theta $$

Example: Express a force of 10 N acting at 40° to the horizontal in terms of horizontal and vertical components.

Horizontal Component:

$$ F_1 = 10 \times \cos(40^\circ) \approx 7.66 \text{ N} $$

Vertical Component:

$$ F_2 = 10 \times \sin(40^\circ) \approx 6.43 \text{ N} $$

Result in component form: $$ \mathbf{F} = (7.66\mathbf{i} + 6.43\mathbf{j}) \text{ N} $$

Example: Vector $$\mathbf{a}$$ has magnitude 10. Vector $$\mathbf{b}$$ has magnitude 20 and is at an angle of 30° to $$\mathbf{a}$$. Find the magnitude of $$\mathbf{a}+\mathbf{b}$$ using the triangle rule.

Step 1: Draw the vector triangle. By placing the vectors tip-to-tail, the angle inside the triangle between them is $$180^\circ - 30^\circ = 150^\circ$$.

Step 2: Use the Cosine Rule to find the magnitude of the resultant vector.

$$ |\mathbf{a}+\mathbf{b}|^2 = |\mathbf{a}|^2 + |\mathbf{b}|^2 - 2|\mathbf{a}||\mathbf{b}|\cos(150^\circ) $$ $$ |\mathbf{a}+\mathbf{b}|^2 = 10^2 + 20^2 - 2(10)(20)\cos(150^\circ) $$ $$ |\mathbf{a}+\mathbf{b}|^2 = 100 + 400 - 400(-0.866) = 846.41 $$ $$ |\mathbf{a}+\mathbf{b}| = \sqrt{846.41} \approx 29.1 $$

Parallelogram Rule

How it Works

An alternative rule for adding two vectors. Place the start points of vectors $$\mathbf{a}$$ and $$\mathbf{b}$$ together, then complete the parallelogram. The diagonal of the parallelogram from the starting point represents the sum of $$\mathbf{a}$$ and $$\mathbf{b}$$.

Example: An object is acted upon by two forces. One is 10N acting horizontally to the right. The other is 20N acting at 60° to the horizontal to the left. Find the resultant force R.

Step 1: Draw the parallelogram. The angle between the two force vectors is $$180^\circ - 60^\circ = 120^\circ$$. In the vector triangle used for calculation, the corresponding angle is $$180^\circ - 120^\circ = 60^\circ$$.

Step 2: Use the Cosine Rule to find the magnitude of R.

$$ |\mathbf{R}|^2 = 10^2 + 20^2 - 2(10)(20)\cos(60^\circ) $$ $$ |\mathbf{R}|^2 = 100 + 400 - 400(0.5) = 300 $$ $$ |\mathbf{R}| = \sqrt{300} \approx 17.3 \text{ N} $$

Step 3: Use the Cosine Rule again to find the direction.

Let $$\theta$$ be the angle between the resultant R and the 20N force.

$$ \cos\theta = \frac{|\mathbf{R}|^2 + 20^2 - 10^2}{2 \times |\mathbf{R}| \times 20} = \frac{300 + 400 - 100}{2 \times \sqrt{300} \times 20} = \frac{600}{692.8} \approx 0.866 $$ $$ \theta = \cos^{-1}(0.866) = 30^\circ $$

Resolving Vectors in 3D

When to Use

In 3D, a vector $$\mathbf{F}$$ with magnitude $$|\mathbf{F}|$$ and direction angles $$\alpha, \beta, \gamma$$ (to x, y, z axes) has components:

$$ F_x = |\mathbf{F}|\cos\alpha, \quad F_y = |\mathbf{F}|\cos\beta, \quad F_z = |\mathbf{F}|\cos\gamma $$

Note: $$\cos^2\alpha + \cos^2\beta + \cos^2\gamma = 1$$. This is essential for 3D force analysis in mechanical structures.

Example: A force of 50 N acts in a direction where $$\cos\alpha = 0.6$$, $$\cos\beta = 0.8$$, $$\cos\gamma = 0$$. Find the components.

$$ F_x = 50 \times 0.6 = 30 \text{ N}, \quad F_y = 50 \times 0.8 = 40 \text{ N}, \quad F_z = 50 \times 0 = 0 \text{ N} $$

Position Vectors, Unit Vectors & Direction Cosines

Position Vectors

The vector $$\vec{OP} = \mathbf{r}$$ specifies the position of a point P relative to the origin O and is called the position vector of P. Using the triangle rule, it can be expressed in terms of its components along the axes.

For a point P(a,b,c), the position vector is given by:

$$ \mathbf{r} = a\mathbf{i} + b\mathbf{j} + c\mathbf{k} $$

Direction Cosines

The direction cosines of a vector are the cosines of the angles the vector makes with the x, y, and z axes ($$\alpha, \beta, \gamma$$ respectively). They are also the components of the unit vector in the direction of the main vector.

$$ \cos\alpha = \frac{a}{|\mathbf{r}|}, \quad \cos\beta = \frac{b}{|\mathbf{r}|}, \quad \cos\gamma = \frac{c}{|\mathbf{r}|} $$

Example: The position vector of a point is P(4,7,0). Determine the magnitude of the vector, its direction cosines, and the direction angles.

Step 1: Write the vector in component form and find its magnitude.
The vector is $$ \mathbf{r} = 4\mathbf{i} + 7\mathbf{j} + 0\mathbf{k} $$.

$$ |\mathbf{r}| = \sqrt{4^2 + 7^2 + 0^2} = \sqrt{16 + 49} = \sqrt{65} \approx 8.06 $$

Step 2: Find the direction cosines.
The direction cosines are the components of the unit vector $$\hat{\mathbf{r}}$$.

$$ \cos\alpha = \frac{4}{\sqrt{65}}, \quad \cos\beta = \frac{7}{\sqrt{65}}, \quad \cos\gamma = \frac{0}{\sqrt{65}} = 0 $$

Step 3: Find the direction angles ($$\alpha$$, $$\beta$$, $$\gamma$$) by taking the inverse cosine.

$$ \alpha = \arccos\left(\frac{4}{\sqrt{65}}\right) \approx 60.26^\circ $$
$$ \beta = \arccos\left(\frac{7}{\sqrt{65}}\right) \approx 29.74^\circ $$
$$ \gamma = \arccos(0) = 90^\circ $$

Applications in Mechanical Engineering

Key Uses

  • Forces in Structures: Resolving forces in beams or trusses to find equilibrium.
  • Velocity and Acceleration: Analyzing motion in mechanisms, such as robotic arms or vehicles.
  • Moments and Torque: Using position vectors to calculate moments about a point.

Example: In a crane, a force of 100 N is applied at a point with position vector $$\mathbf{r} = 2\mathbf{i} + 3\mathbf{j} + 1\mathbf{k}$$ m from the pivot. If the force direction is along $$\mathbf{f} = 1\mathbf{i} + 1\mathbf{j} + 1\mathbf{k}$$, find the unit vector of the force and its components.

First, find the magnitude of $$\mathbf{f}$$:

$$ |\mathbf{f}| = \sqrt{1^2+1^2+1^2} = \sqrt{3} $$

Unit vector:

$$ \hat{\mathbf{f}} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{3}}\mathbf{i} + \frac{1}{\sqrt{3}}\mathbf{j} + \frac{1}{\sqrt{3}}\mathbf{k} $$

Force vector:

$$ \mathbf{F} = 100 \hat{\mathbf{f}} = \frac{100}{\sqrt{3}} (\mathbf{i} + \mathbf{j} + \mathbf{k}) \approx 57.74 (\mathbf{i} + \mathbf{j} + \mathbf{k}) \text{ N} $$

Vector Products (Dot and Cross)

Dot Product

The dot product of $$\mathbf{a}$$ and $$\mathbf{b}$$ is a scalar:

$$ \mathbf{a} \cdot \mathbf{b} = |\mathbf{a}| |\mathbf{b}| \cos\theta = a_x b_x + a_y b_y + a_z b_z $$

Used for work done by a force ($$ W = \mathbf{F} \cdot \mathbf{d} $$) or projecting vectors.

Example: $$\mathbf{a} = 2\mathbf{i} + 3\mathbf{j}$$, $$\mathbf{b} = 4\mathbf{i} - 1\mathbf{j}$$. Find $$\mathbf{a} \cdot \mathbf{b}$$

$$ \mathbf{a} \cdot \mathbf{b} = (2)(4) + (3)(-1) = 8 - 3 = 5 $$

Cross Product

The cross product is a vector: $$ \mathbf{a} \times \mathbf{b} = |\mathbf{a}| |\mathbf{b}| \sin\theta \hat{\mathbf{n}} $$, where $$\hat{\mathbf{n}}$$ is perpendicular to both.

In components:

$$ \mathbf{a} \times \mathbf{b} = (a_y b_z - a_z b_y)\mathbf{i} - (a_x b_z - a_z b_x)\mathbf{j} + (a_x b_y - a_y b_x)\mathbf{k} $$

Used for torque ($$ \mathbf{\tau} = \mathbf{r} \times \mathbf{F} $$) in mechanical systems.

Example: $$\mathbf{a} = 2\mathbf{i} + 3\mathbf{j} + 1\mathbf{k}$$, $$\mathbf{b} = 4\mathbf{i} - 1\mathbf{j} + 5\mathbf{k}$$. Find $$\mathbf{a} \times \mathbf{b}$$

$$ \mathbf{a} \times \mathbf{b} = (3\cdot5 - 1\cdot(-1))\mathbf{i} - (2\cdot5 - 1\cdot4)\mathbf{j} + (2\cdot(-1) - 3\cdot4)\mathbf{k} = (15 + 1)\mathbf{i} - (10 - 4)\mathbf{j} + (-2 - 12)\mathbf{k} = 16\mathbf{i} - 6\mathbf{j} - 14\mathbf{k} $$

Area of a Triangle using Vectors

Geometric Interpretation

A powerful application of the cross product is finding the area of a triangle. The magnitude of the cross product, $$|\vec{a} \times \vec{b}|$$, represents the area of the parallelogram formed by vectors $$\vec{a}$$ and $$\vec{b}$$. Since a triangle with the same two vectors as adjacent sides is exactly half of this parallelogram, its area is straightforward to calculate.

For a triangle with vertices $P$, $Q$, and $R$, we define two vectors originating from the same vertex, such as $$\vec{PQ}$$ and $$\vec{PR}$$. The area is given by the formula:

$$ \text{Area} = \frac{1}{2} |\vec{PQ} \times \vec{PR}| $$

Steps to Calculate the Area

  1. Form Vectors: Choose one vertex as the starting point (e.g., P) and create two vectors to the other vertices ($$\vec{PQ}$$ and $$\vec{PR}$$).
  2. Calculate Cross Product: Compute the cross product of these two vectors ($$\vec{PQ} \times \vec{PR}$$).
  3. Find the Magnitude: Calculate the magnitude of the resulting vector.
  4. Determine the Area: The area of the triangle is exactly half of this magnitude.

Example: Determine the area of the triangle with vertices $A(1, 1)$, $B(5, 3)$, and $C(3, 9)$.

Even though the points are 2D, we treat them as 3D vectors with a z-component of 0 to enable the cross product. So, $A=(1,1,0)$, $B=(5,3,0)$, and $C=(3,9,0)$.

1. Form Vectors: Let's choose vertex A as our origin.

$$ \vec{AB} = B - A = (5-1)\mathbf{i} + (3-1)\mathbf{j} = 4\mathbf{i} + 2\mathbf{j} $$ $$ \vec{AC} = C - A = (3-1)\mathbf{i} + (9-1)\mathbf{j} = 2\mathbf{i} + 8\mathbf{j} $$

2. Calculate Cross Product: Now, compute $$\vec{AB} \times \vec{AC}$$.

$$ \vec{AB} \times \vec{AC} = \begin{vmatrix} \mathbf{i} & \mathbf{j} & \mathbf{k} \\ 4 & 2 & 0 \\ 2 & 8 & 0 \end{vmatrix} = (2 \cdot 0 - 8 \cdot 0)\mathbf{i} - (4 \cdot 0 - 2 \cdot 0)\mathbf{j} + (4 \cdot 8 - 2 \cdot 2)\mathbf{k} $$ $$ = 0\mathbf{i} - 0\mathbf{j} + (32 - 4)\mathbf{k} = 28\mathbf{k} $$

3. Find the Magnitude:

$$ |\vec{AB} \times \vec{AC}| = |28\mathbf{k}| = \sqrt{0^2 + 0^2 + 28^2} = 28 $$

4. Determine the Area:

$$ \text{Area of } \triangle ABC = \frac{1}{2} |\vec{AB} \times \vec{AC}| = \frac{1}{2} \times 28 = 14 $$

The area of the triangle is 14 square units.
